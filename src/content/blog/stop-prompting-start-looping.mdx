A line on my feed reorganized how I think about this, which doesn't happen often: stop prompting your coding agent, and start designing the loop that prompts it for you.

"Loop engineering" spread across X in days. Normally I roll my eyes at a freshly coined term doing numbers, but this one earned it. The reframe is small and it actually moves something.

## The shift

When you prompt, you optimize a single exchange: write, read, write the next one, you in the chair the whole time. The unit of value is the *response*: did I get one good answer.

Loop engineering moves the unit to the *trajectory*, the whole act → observe → reason → repeat cycle that runs until a goal is met. A bad turn stops mattering if turn four catches the bug and fixes it. Addy Osmani, who popularized the term, put it plainly: you're replacing yourself as the person who prompts the agent, and designing the system that does it instead.

## What a loop actually is

Underneath the new word, it's not exotic. It cycles through four moves: **perceive** (take in the goal, the last result, the errors), **reason** (figure out what to do next), **act** (run a tool, write code, call an API), and **observe** (read what happened; success moves on, failure feeds back into the next reason step). Repeat until a stop condition is true. That last clause is the entire ballgame. A concrete version: an automation wakes each morning, reads the CI failures, opens isolated worktrees so fixes don't collide, has sub-agents draft and review, opens PRs, and drops anything unresolved into an inbox for you. You didn't prompt a single step. You built the thing that prompts.

## The part the viral version skips: the bill

A loop with a fuzzy stop condition doesn't finish, it grinds, and grinding costs money. The failure modes are well documented and a little alarming:

- Loops that never decide they're done.
- Context windows that fill until reasoning degrades.
- Silent failures that report confidence with zero progress.
- One writeup describes an agent making 400 tool calls in five minutes.

And the meter is real. A single agent burns roughly 4x the tokens of a normal chat; multi-agent setups around 15x. There's a documented case that hit **$1.3 million in monthly token usage.** "I'll just let it run" is how you find that out the expensive way.

## What separates a good loop from a runaway

- **Independent verification.** The agent that wrote the code doesn't grade its own work; a separate check decides whether the goal is met.
- **Hard bounds.** Iteration caps, token budgets, an exit when the state stops changing.
- **A human on the irreversible steps.** Auto-merging a PR is a terrible place to learn your verifier was wrong.

What actually nags at me isn't the cost, though. It's quieter: the faster a loop ships code you didn't write, the wider the gap between what's in your repo and what you understand, and that gap comes due during the next incident. The real trap is the comfortable one: accept whatever it produced, hit go again, stop reading.

So build the loop. The people getting the most out of AI right now genuinely aren't writing cleverer prompts, they're building loops that finish. Just build it, in Osmani's words, like someone who intends to stay the engineer, not the person who presses go and walks off.
